About The Founders

The Socratic Exchange was founded by Leo Cao (President) and Josh Tran (Vice President) to elevate discourse in business and economics through candid, long-form conversation.

We share stories, challenges, and ideas with scholars and industry leaders—offering unfiltered insight that goes beyond headlines.

Join us as we build a community of curious minds committed to rigor, ethics, and real-world impact.

Our Educational Mission

The Socratic Exchange is a cutting edge incubator and seminar grounded in the foundational ideas of economic and philosophical thinkers. From the moral sentiments of Adam Smith to Malthus’ theory on the limits to economic growth, our seminars aim to bridge nuanced business theories and real-world applications. By engaging students with professors and entrepreneurs from Stanford, Berkeley, USC, and Cal Haas, we drive students to develop their critical thinking skills towards meaningful innovations—all in 6 weeks.

Course Highlights:

  • Core Economic Principles: From macro to micro.
  • Entrepreneurial Strategy: Market analysis and validation.
  • Leadership & Ethics: Building sustainable and responsible ventures.

The Gentle Power of Weak Ties: Lessons from a Conversation with Mark Granovetter

By Leo Cao

“You never know which one it’s going to be.” — Mark Granovetter, on the weak tie that might change your life

In 1973, Stanford sociologist Mark Granovetter published a paper with a title that sounds deceptively simple: The Strength of Weak Ties. Fifty years and over 76,000 citations later, that phrase has become something of a sacred koan in the social sciences — repeated in classrooms, startup accelerators, and policy think tanks alike. But what does it really mean? And what happens when a high school student hears it for the first time and asks: Could that idea change my life?

This July, we sat down with Dr. Granovetter — not in a lecture hall, but across a screen, in a modest Zoom call recorded only through transcript. He spoke with us, two high schoolers from San Jose, not as if we were simply students, but as curious interlocutors deserving of answers.

Weak Ties in a Digitized World

Granovetter’s central claim, now canonized, is that “weak ties”—the looser, more casual relationships in our lives—often act as crucial bridges to opportunity. Unlike strong ties (close friends, family), weak ties occupy different social circles. They know things we don’t. They introduce us to people we wouldn’t otherwise meet. And they often become the invisible scaffolding behind major life shifts—new jobs, new ventures, new ideas.

At the time, the theory disrupted how economists and sociologists understood networks and labor markets. But it’s the digital age that has truly confirmed its brilliance. “Weak ties have actually become more important,” Granovetter told us, “because it’s so easy to make them.” Social platforms like LinkedIn and Instagram, he noted, haven’t made his theory obsolete—they’ve made it urgent.

Where previous generations relied on Rolodexes and face-to-face meetings, today’s youth carry hundreds of potential bridges in their pockets. The challenge now isn’t access, but intention: how to cultivate authenticity in an age of manufactured connection.

On Networking: Be Human First

When we asked for networking advice—perhaps expecting a silver bullet—Granovetter paused. “I don’t think I ever actually tried to network,” he said. “Figure out who you’re really compatible with… not contacts in the sense of people you can use, but people you actually connect with.”

This is not the voice of someone optimizing for a better LinkedIn funnel. Granovetter’s wisdom came from decades of sociological study. And still, he found that trust, empathy, and sustained curiosity were still the most powerful currencies in any system.

He warned us, gently, of the “grotesqueness” of transactional networking events. The kind where business cards are handed out like Halloween candy, where eyes dart over your shoulder in search of someone more useful. “Some people are pretty good at pretending,” he said. “Others… you just want to run in the other direction.” That is to say, the strongest networks are organic.

AI, Algorithms, and the Vanishing Human Touch

In one of the more sobering moments of our conversation, we asked Granovetter about trust in the digital age. In his view, economic life is always embedded in social life. But what happens when algorithms replace handshakes, and reviews are written by chatbots? Are we losing something essential?

He didn’t rush to condemn technology, but he did point to a growing loneliness—a world where older adults find comfort in AI companions and digital reviews shape buying behavior more than human recommendations. “It’s a little weird, a little sad,” he said. “But if that’s the best they can do, it’s better than nothing.”

Connection used to be earned through shared experience. Now it’s often outsourced to code. The question we left with wasn’t just how to build better businesses, but how to build better relationships inside them.

The Philosophy of Academic Writing

Perhaps the most unexpected takeaway was about writing. When we told Dr. Granovetter that his academic prose was—unusually—readable, he laughed and credited his background in history. Historians, he said, are trained to write for people, not just professors. “Most academic writing is terrible,” he admitted. “But you're supposed to write in a way that people can understand. Just obvious.”

It wasn’t lost on us that the sociologist who changed how the world thinks about relationships also wanted his writing to feel like a conversation. There’s an ethical conviction in that. Accessibility, after all, is a form of respect.

From Princeton to Palo Alto—and to Us

Mark Granovetter didn’t have to meet with us. He’s retired. He’s already reshaped economic sociology. He’s done the lectures, written the book (The Sociology of Economic Life), and mentored a generation of scholars. And yet, on a quiet summer day, he gave two rising high school seniors over an hour of his time—patiently explaining Markov chains, academic politics, and the tragic absence of royalties for sociologists.

What we left with was not just a deeper understanding of weak ties, but a clearer vision of what it means to live a strong life: one rooted in genuine curiosity, intellectual discipline, and human decency.

At the end of the interview, Granovetter commented on the intellectual drive of The Socratic Exchange and its members: “I could tell from Leo’s message that he was unusually intellectually curious—really unusually so, especially for someone his age,” Granovetter said. “That’s why I wanted to talk.”

In a world obsessed with scaling, Mark Granovetter reminded us to stay grounded in a life rooted in genuine curiosity, intellectual discipline, and human decency. Because while your next opportunity may come from a weak tie—your legacy comes from how you treat the people who expect nothing from you.

Leo Cao is a founder of The Socratic Exchange, a student-led seminar series exploring the intersection of business, ethics, and philosophy. He’s currently a senior at Evergreen Valley High School.
